Industry News

The VC landscape is bifurcating — and knowing which half you're in changes your entire strategy.

Venture Capital

"Less than 3% of venture capital funding goes to women — that's what I wanted to fix"

CTech · 5h ago

A founder-turned-fund-manager built a venture vehicle to address a gap that has held at roughly 3% of all VC deployed for years — despite widespread industry attention and no sign of self-correction. She made the shift from operator to allocator because the structural reasons are clear: traditional VC will not rebalance without deliberate capital reallocation from people who have lived the rejection. For B2B founders building in spaces where 97% of the money is chasing companies that don't look like theirs, this is a direct map of who is building the alternative capital infrastructure. The open question is whether emerging funds like hers can reach the scale needed to move the aggregate number, or whether they remain a precision correction inside a much larger broken system.

Claude's Take
The stat itself is old news — what's new is that the founders experiencing the gap are now building the fix from the capital side. That structural shift matters: the next generation of women-focused funds will be run by people who were the rejected pitch, not philanthropists.

Little Pursuits
You Don't Know How Stressed You Are.
Chronic founder stress is not primarily a management problem — it is a measurement problem. The body recalibrates to sustained pressure until what used to feel alarming feels like a normal Tuesday. Two RCT-backed mechanisms help: HRV biofeedback and objective self-monitoring with accelerometers, not self-report. The actionable frame: pick one objective signal, watch it without interpreting it for 30 days, and recruit one person with explicit permission to tell you the truth.
By the time a founder can feel the cost of chronic stress, they've already paid most of it — because the instrument they'd use to measure it has been quietly recalibrated by the very environment they're trying to evaluate.

a16z Speedrun
Early Stage Markets Rarely Look Like Markets
Markets begin as behavioral shifts in small, overlooked communities — not legible categories with sector maps. By the time a market is mappable, the best entry points have already closed. The signals to look for: intensity of behavior in a small group, early community that sustains itself without the company's involvement, and a small group spending surprising amounts of time in ways the founders didn't plan for. The practical method: go extremely deep with ten users before going broad.
If you're waiting for your market to have a name before you build in it, you're already late — the best entry points close before the sector map exists.

Melissa Kwan
Making money is a skill
Naval Ravikant's framing — "making money is not a thing you do, it's a skill you learn" — applied to fifteen years of founder journey. The only skill she went deep on was sales. Everything else — marketing, customer success, operations, networking — she dabbled in rather than mastered. The reframe removes victimhood: financial underperformance isn't about where you came from, it's about skills not yet acquired. And skills can be learned.
The gap between where you are and where you want to be financially is almost always a skills gap disguised as a circumstance gap — and the sooner you name it as the former, the sooner you can close it.

American Gothic

Wood painted his sister and his dentist as the models — not a married couple. The woman looks slightly to the side, the man straight at you. When it was first shown, Iowans were furious. Wood thought he was painting American determination. Everyone else saw something else.

Plate IV  ·  Oil on canvas
The Child's Bath
Mary Cassatt  ·  1893

Cassatt was the only American in the Impressionist circle. She never married. She painted women and children — not because she was sentimental, but because they were the only subjects she could access without a chaperone. This is about intimacy without sentiment.
