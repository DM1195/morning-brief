Plate I  ·  Oil on canvas
Early Morning, Tarpon Springs
George Inness  ·  1892

There is something almost accidental about the figure in this painting — a man so absorbed into the Florida dawn that the color of his trousers mirrors the tree trunks around him, as if he is being slowly claimed by the grove. What surprises is that Inness didn’t paint nature for its beauty but for its theology: a follower of Swedenborgian Christianity, he believed every tree and shadow was suffused with divine meaning, making this misty sunrise a site of genuine spiritual practice, not just picturesque scenery. The painting reveals how radically private an act of looking can be — one person, standing still in pink and blue light, disappearing into something larger than himself.

Plate II  ·  Oil on canvas
Flower Girl in Holland
George Hitchcock  ·  1887

Hitchcock did something quietly audacious here: he lived in the Dutch village of Egmond for twenty years and painted its flower sellers not as exotic subjects but as neighbors, their familiar faces set against landscapes he quietly edited — removing nearby houses, opening up a bucolic horizon that didn’t quite exist. Though his modeling of figures was rigorously academic, critics called him a daring colorist anyway, because those brilliant hues and open brushstrokes felt like a kind of recklessness against the careful draftsmanship underneath. This painting lives in that tension — between discipline and boldness, between the woman who is really there and the world Hitchcock decided she deserved to stand in.

Plate III  ·  Oil on canvas
On a Balcony
Mary Cassatt  ·  1878–79

Most people assume Mary Cassatt’s domestic scenes are about intimacy and retreat — but look at what the woman in this garden is reading: a newspaper, not a novel, connecting her deliberately to the world outside the balcony’s blue rail even as she sits inside it. Shown at the 1880 Impressionist exhibition, the painting used energetic bursts of red, blue, yellow, and pink not just for visual effect but as a signal of modernity — that middle-class women, even in morning dress, in enclosed gardens, were participants in contemporary life. What the work quietly insists on is that private space and public awareness were never opposites.

Plate IV  ·  Pastel over monotype on cream laid paper
Ballet at the Paris Opéra
Edgar Degas  ·  1877

Degas built this image on one of the widest monotype plates he ever used, then layered pastel — described by contemporaries as ‘the powder of butterfly wings’ — over the inked surface, creating something that exists between print and painting, between a fixed moment and something alive and fragile. The detail that stops you: this is almost certainly a rehearsal, not a performance — the dancers’ hair is loose, their positions casual, and the double basses intrude from the orchestra pit below as if the boundary between audience and stage has quietly dissolved. What it reveals is that Degas was less interested in the spectacle of ballet than in the unguarded, unglamorous truth of what it cost.
