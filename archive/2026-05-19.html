STARTUP FUNDRAISING VENTURE CAPITAL

Venture Capital Is Concentrating Faster Than Ever. What Happens To Everyone Else?

CRUNCHBASE NEWS · Today

In 2025, 70% of all U.S. venture capital went to just 389 companies raising $100 million or more — with $90 billion alone absorbed by six companies that each cleared $5 billion in a single raise. Through April 2026, the trend is accelerating: 80% of available capital has gone to 29 companies raising $500 million and up, leaving roughly 6,000 startups to divide what remains. The round that used to bridge early-stage founders to growth-stage — the $100M–$500M tier — has effectively collapsed as a competitive market, with 770 companies competing for it in 2021 versus a fraction today. Investors on the ground still talk up seed and Series A as vibrant white space, but that framing papers over the structural reality: the ladder between early promise and real scale is gone for most non-AI founders.

Claude's Take
The VC ladder is broken for most founders — this isn't a cycle, it's structural redistribution toward AI incumbents. If you're building B2B SaaS, price your strategy around reaching profitability or acquisition readiness before a Series A, not raising one.

Plate I  ·  Oil on canvas
Mountain Brook
Albert Bierstadt  ·  1863

When critics called this Bierstadt's "best work" in 1863, what they were praising was a composed lie — the Flume in New Hampshire rendered as imaginary wilderness, a tourist attraction transformed into untouched nature myth. The painting's realism is total but its subject is invented, assembled from landscape elements Bierstadt rearranged the way a writer edits a sentence. He was doing for the White Mountains what we now do with photography: a selective edit of experience so convincing it becomes more real than the thing it depicts.

Plate II  ·  Oil on canvas
Pastoral Landscape with Ruins
Adriaen van de Velde  ·  1664

A Dutch painter who never went to Italy painted the Italian countryside from imagination, and his patrons believed it entirely — because the fantasy of rural peace is more durable than any actual landscape. Van de Velde built this from cattle sketches made in Amsterdam pastures and studio studies of human figures, then assembled the scene like a stage set. The Roman ruins exist; the warm southern light is invented; and the whole thing is true in the way all good idealization is true — it shows you what you wish were real, and that feeling passes for memory.

Plate III  ·  Oil on canvas
Water Lilies
Claude Monet  ·  1906

Monet called the water lily paintings "an obsession" — not a compliment to himself. Technical examination reveals he repainted this canvas many times; what looks like effortless immediacy is the residue of relentless revision. He was eliminating the sky and horizon deliberately, trying to remove the reference points that tell you which way is up — so that standing in front of it, you lose your orientation slightly. That was the point: a painting that floats, that refuses to give you stable ground, that asks you to surrender spatial certainty before it will show you anything.

Plate IV  ·  Oil on canvas
The Song of the Lark
Jules Breton  ·  1884

She's stopped mid-field at dawn to hear a lark she cannot see, and Breton gives her the same golden light he gives the sky — she is continuous with the horizon, not standing in front of it. Breton grew up in Courrieres and painted his village people for a Paris market that found the rural poor romantically appealing; this painting traveled to the American Midwest, where Willa Cather encountered it and used it as the central image of her 1915 novel about a girl who becomes an opera singer. That transformation — peasant girl, painting, novel, opera — is proof that art made about small moments can carry enormous ambitions across very long distances.
