AI POLICY REGULATION

The U.S. has 1,200 AI bills and no good test for any of them

Fortune · Yesterday

More than 1,200 active AI-related bills now crowd federal and state legislatures, with roughly 150 already enacted — yet no agreed-upon standard exists for evaluating whether any of them constitute good policy. Researchers Sonnenfeld, Marcus, and Henriques propose a three-stage diagnostic: check whether existing law already covers the harm, run a cost-benefit analysis across precision, breadth, and enforceability, then assess whether the rule can survive implementation. States like Colorado and Utah passed sweeping omnibus measures only to narrow them within months, revealing that even their own drafters weren't confident the frameworks would hold. For B2B founders building AI-adjacent software, the practical implication is structural: audit trails, incident reporting, and documented safety frameworks will become standard procurement requirements as these bills converge — and companies building those capabilities now will have a compliance moat that late-movers cannot easily replicate.

Claude's Take
This is a moat-building moment disguised as a regulatory headache. The companies that instrument their AI systems with audit logs, incident reporting, and documented safety frameworks today will become the default enterprise choice when procurement teams start requiring compliance documentation — and that moment is 12 to 18 months away at most.

John Singer Sargent  ·  1897

Sargent painted Elsie Swinton — a celebrated amateur singer about to go professional — with exactly the tension that defined her life: a face of composed realism dissolving into impressionist bursts of shimmering fabric at her shoulder, as if the paint couldn't decide whether to pin her down or set her free. Daring Ventures
The Midnight Invoice
ServiceTitan's $6.3B IPO traces back to one founding insight: every other home services platform built for the homeowner, but Mahdessian and Kuzoyan built for the contractor — because their fathers were contractors. The consumer-facing platforms diagnosed the right market but the wrong customer, treating contractors as supply rather than building the operating system around which contractor revenue actually moves. ServiceTitan became mission-critical by following the full workflow — intake to dispatch to invoice to payment to membership — until it processed $82B in gross transaction volume with 95%+ gross dollar retention.
The most visible customer pain in a market is almost never the one that becomes a durable operating system — the contractor, not the homeowner, was the platform.

Product with Attitude
How I Built Product with Attitude from 0 to Bestseller, without Pretending I Have a Viral Playbook
Karo Zieminski grew from 0 to 18,000 readers and Substack Bestseller in six months by treating the newsletter as a product — with a person (a defined reader mindset), a promise (a single sentence readers would use to defend their subscription), and a feedback loop that distinguishes signal from noise. Most newsletters fail as products because they deliver content without delivering a repeatable experience of value. The key insight: feedback signals split into loud/public (likes, restacks) vs. quiet/high-value (DMs, phrases readers quote back), and writers over-index on the former while ignoring the signals that actually indicate whether the promise is working.
A newsletter's promise should live above its practicalities — not format, frequency, or topic — just the sentence a reader would use to justify their subscription to a skeptical friend.

After the Bullfight
Mary Cassatt  ·  1873

Cassatt painted a bullfighter at rest — not mid-charge, not heroic — and in doing so pulled off a quiet subversion: the most theatrical figure in Spain rendered in an off-moment, bravado softened to something almost domestic. She was in Seville to study the Old Masters, following the path Manet had recently traveled, but her instinct was to capture the man behind the costume rather than the spectacle. The thick paint and rich color do the work the crowd would have done.

Coast of Maine
Winslow Homer  ·  1893

Homer moved to Prouts Neck in near-isolation in 1883 and spent a decade watching the shoreline — these seascapes carry no human figures because the sea itself was the subject, not its backdrop. The diagonal shoreline creates a physical sensation of instability, the rocks simultaneously solid and swept. What reads almost abstract in the flat passages of color is Homer compressing time: every tide he'd watched, collapsed into a single unchanging moment.
