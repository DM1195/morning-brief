Industry News

VENTURE CAPITAL

“Less than 3% of venture capital funding goes to women — that’s what I wanted to fix”

CTech  ·  Yesterday

A founder-turned-fund-manager profiles her decision to build a venture vehicle specifically targeting the persistent gap in capital for women-led startups — a figure that has held at under 3% of all VC deployed for years, surviving multiple cycles of industry hand-wringing without structural change. Her argument is that the fix cannot come from inside existing funds because the incentive structure of traditional VC rewards pattern-matching, and most patterns were set by a decade of male-founded companies that exited. For a solo founder building B2B SaaS, this is a direct map of who is actively building the supply side, not just commenting on the gap. The open question is whether these emerging founder-run funds can aggregate enough LP capital to actually move the aggregate statistic, or whether they remain a boutique correction inside a much larger, still-broken system.

Claude's Take
The 3% figure is no longer just a criticism — it’s becoming a founding thesis for a whole wave of new funds run by people who were the rejected pitch. That is a more durable structural change than any diversity pledge from an existing firm. Pay attention to who is raising right now.

Substack: “The 3% Problem Isn’t Going to Fix Itself — So Someone Is Building Around It” — frame through the lens of a solo B2B founder watching a new capital infrastructure get built in real time, and what it means for her own fundraising strategy.

Not Boring by Packy McCormick
Riding the Leopard
Drawing on Shannon’s information theory, Packy builds a case that differentiation is a moral obligation — not just a personal preference. Shannon proved mathematically that information is surprise, meaning a universe of identical people produces no new information. The conclusion: being the fullest, strangest version of yourself is what the universe requires of you.
Differentiation is a moral obligation — the universe learns something through you only to the extent that you are the unexpected thing only you could be.
Content angle: Use Shannon’s information theory to argue that fitting in destroys the very thing that makes your content worth reading.
